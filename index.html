Personal Statement

What sets me apart from most people I have had the opportunity to meet is my interdisciplinary background and interest in fusing STEAM disciplines for education. My lifelong interest in science took me into the molecular biology lab, my desire for creative expression took me into the ceramics lab, and my interest in technology and visual media took me into the computer lab. I think this breadth and depth of experience at the intersection of science, media, and technology shapes the way I approach problem solving and education in a positive, beneficial way that I wouldn't have had I not been involved in these different areas.

What shifted my thinking from science to STEAM was exposure to coursework that I thought would be much better served through animation and visual experiences versus academic reporting. With the permission of a course professor, I submitted an assignment as a media file instead of the requested paper and received excellent feedback from fellow students and the instructor on finding a method for creatively addressing a tangled biochemical pathway we were to explore in class. In addition, I was encouraged to get involved in a national outreach program at NASA Kennedy Space Center and ended up working for a summer with undergraduate students in the Life Science Laboratory there. This further spurred my interest in outreach and education.

In graduate school I worked as a teaching assistant for a variety of biology and botany courses while completing requirements for my PhD in technology. I had some opportunities to hone my instructional skills and was awarded a Fellowship from the National Science Foundation GK-12 program to bring my expertise to a middle school science and mathematics classroom. In that role I worked with a teacher to develop and publish a lesson on social networks and a simulated virus transmission among the student population.

After school I worked in a role that provided myriad opportunities for growth and creative curriculum development. As part of that team I helped launch and develop K-12 STEAM programs related to video production, 3D-printing, and interactive tutorials. I was deeply involved in teacher professional development and led a series of workshops where I was able to implement novel instruction for teachers to learn to use statistical analysis software in their classrooms. My goals were to increase teacher effectiveness and to create highly-engaging media for both formal and informal educational experiences.

Perhaps the most important trait I offer is that of collaboration. I previously built a collaborative network from the ground up to enable our group to produce a large library of video content which relied on partnerships from local, municipal, state, and national partners. I'm confident in saying I played a very important role in establishing, fostering, and leveraging those connections to allow us to produce content to help teachers. What I gained from that experience is a deep interest in bridging fields across STEM disciplines and helping educators through those connections.

My future interests lie in continuing to find opportunities at the crossroads of science, technology, education, and visual media to promote learning in traditional and non-traditional environments. I'm especially interested in the future of computational thinking in the classroom and applications for data science and visualization within K-12 schools.

Related Research

Biosensor-controlled Interactive Environments for Virtual Reality | PhD coursework
Collaborated on an interactive and immersive VR environment involving user biofeedback to control a helicopter in an outdoor scene. My colleague created the hardware interface while I created the models, environment, and interactions. A stretch sensor worn around the abdomen allowed the user to drive several inputs for environmental lighting, helicopter altitude, and on-craft lighting.

Evaluation of Dead-reckoning Ability in Immersive Virtual Environments | PhD coursework
Collaborated on a simple VR testing environment and corresponding research program to evaluate users' ability to determine distances and positions in a flat scene with several levels of navigational cues. Users were scored on their ability to travel in a prescribed manner using temporary targets and to then return to the origin.

Influence of Interactive Spatial Problem Solving on Spatial Test Performance | PhD coursework
Developed a pilot test to examine the ability of interactive spatial problem solving within game play to influence user scores on standardized spatial ability tests. Users completed game play in between pre-test and post-test evaluation.

Visuospatial Characterization and Analysis of Spatial Abilities of Video Game Players | PhD dissertation
Addressed the taxonomy associated with games used in spatial ability research as well as the connection between game play experience and spatial test performance across groups of players by dominant genre. Self-reported participant data were used to classify games by inherent visuospatial characteristics. These characteristics were then used to cluster users with preferred gameplay. Clusters were then compared to determine differences, if any, across three measures of spatial ability.

Soybean Growth Response to Touch | Horticulture Research
This video was recorded in a vibration-free darkroom illuminated by special green lighting designed to not stimulate growth. A watch was placed to aid in time measurement. As the seedlings emerge, they lengthen through expansion of cells in the hypocotyl elongation zone at a similar rate. At the time of touch, noted by the removal on an extra seedling in the front, one seedling (this one got poked) slows to a halt, while the other continues to elongate. Bonus: you can see the seedlings exhibiting nutation (bending back and forth) as they elongate in this time-lapse video.
